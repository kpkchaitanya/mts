# MTS Pedagogical Guidelines

**Organization:** Masters Tuition Services LLC
**Version:** v1
**Status:** Active

---

## 1. Purpose

This document captures the instructional design principles that govern how
MTS educational content is structured and sequenced.

It exists to ensure that system-generated content reflects how MTS actually teaches —
not just what standard worksheets look like.

---

## 2. Core Instructional Philosophy

**Understanding over memorization.**

Content must build conceptual understanding.
Procedures are taught with the reasoning behind them.
Repetition supports fluency, not just recall.

---

## 3. Worksheet Design Principles

### 3.1 Warm-Up → Core Practice → Challenge Arc

Every well-structured worksheet follows this flow:

| Phase | Difficulty | Purpose |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Warm-Up (20–25%) | Below grade level | Activate prior knowledge; build confidence |
| Core Practice (50–60%) | On grade level | Reinforce target standard |
| Challenge (15–25%) | Above grade level | Stretch thinking; differentiate |

### 3.2 No Concept Overload

One primary learning objective per worksheet.
A worksheet on fractions should not introduce integers.
Concept mixing is intentional only when reviewing prior material.

### 3.3 Clear Instructions

Instructions must be:
* One sentence when possible
* In active voice
* Free of jargon the student has not been taught
* Testable — the student knows exactly what to produce

### 3.4 Answer Clarity for Math

For math worksheets:
* Show work spaces (blank lines or boxes beneath problems)
* Answer lines are clearly labeled
* Units are specified where applicable
* Multiple choice (if used) has exactly 4 options with one clearly correct answer

### 3.5 Variety in Problem Format

Within a concept, vary:
* Representation (numerical, word problem, visual)
* Direction (solve for x, evaluate, complete the pattern, error-correct)
* Context (real-world, abstract, student-relevant)

Monotony reduces engagement regardless of content quality.

---

## 4. Answer Key Design Principles

### 4.1 Show Work, Not Just Answers

For math:
* Intermediate steps are shown
* Accepted equivalents are noted (e.g., `1/2` = `0.5`)
* Common errors are pre-noted in margin annotations where helpful

### 4.2 Pedagogical Notes for Teachers

Answer keys may include:
* Suggested follow-up questions if a student gets it wrong
* Common misconceptions to watch for
* Connection to next session's topic

### 4.3 Accuracy is Absolute

An incorrect answer in an answer key is a CRITICAL failure.
Answer keys are QA-gated before any output is finalized.

---

## 5. Grade-Differentiated Formatting

| Grade Band | Format Preferences |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Grades 2–4 | Larger font; more white space; simple numbered list |
| Grades 5–7 | Standard font; moderate density; optional sections |
| Grades 8–10 | Compact; multiple columns acceptable; SAT format when appropriate |

---

## 6. Cognitive Load Management

Do not overload a worksheet with more problems than a student can reasonably
complete in the session time.

| Session Length | Recommended Problem Count (Math) |
|---------------|----------------------------------|
| 60 minutes | 15–25 problems |
| 90 minutes | 20–35 problems |

Quality over quantity. A student who does 10 problems with understanding
learns more than one who rushes through 30 without it.

---

## 7. Pedagogical Anti-Patterns

The following are explicitly NOT MTS style:

| Anti-Pattern | Why |
|-------------|-----|
| Trick questions | Destroy confidence; teach distrust of problems |
| Ambiguous instructions | Create anxiety; reward guessing |
| Unannounced topic changes | Disrupt cognitive organization |
| Density without purpose | Signal that quantity matters more than understanding |
| Answers without reasoning | Reinforce memorization over understanding |
